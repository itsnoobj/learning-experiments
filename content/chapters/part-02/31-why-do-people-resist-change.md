# Why Do People Resist Change Even When It Benefits Them?

> Part II — Understanding Other People · Section A: Incentives & Hidden Motives

---

## The Situation

You're rolling out something objectively better — a new tool, a simpler process, a restructure that gives people more autonomy. You've done the work. The logic is airtight.

And yet. People drag their feet. They find reasons it won't work. They nod in meetings and do nothing after. Some actively sabotage.

You're not fighting the change. You're fighting something deeper.

---

## The Story

In Plato's cave, prisoners have been chained since childhood, facing a blank wall. Behind them, a fire casts shadows of objects carried past. These shadows are all they've ever known — they name them, debate them, build a whole reality around them.

One prisoner is freed. He's dragged up toward the mouth of the cave. The sunlight burns his eyes. Everything is too bright, too overwhelming, too unfamiliar. His first instinct isn't wonder — it's pain. He wants to go back.

Slowly, painfully, his eyes adjust. He sees actual trees, not shadow-trees. Actual people, not flickering shapes. He realizes his entire life was spent studying illusions.

So he goes back. To free the others.

And they think he's insane.

His eyes can't readjust to the dark. He stumbles. He can't name the shadows as quickly as they can. To them, the journey to the light *broke* him. He's proof that leaving is dangerous.

They don't just refuse to go. If anyone tries to drag them out, Plato writes, **they would kill him if they could.**

---

## The Contrast

In the 1990s, Kodak engineers built one of the first digital cameras. They showed it to leadership. The response wasn't excitement — it was burial.

Kodak made its money from film. Everyone in the company — from executives to factory workers — had built their identity, expertise, and daily routines around film. Digital wasn't just a new product. It was a threat to *who they were*.

They didn't lack information. They lacked the willingness to let go of a self-image that was already dying.

Kodak filed for bankruptcy in 2012 — killed not by ignorance, but by the inability to become someone new.

---

## The Principle

**People don't resist change. They resist loss.**

Every change asks someone to give something up — familiarity, competence, status, identity, comfort. The new thing might be better *in the future*, but the loss is felt *right now*. And the brain weighs losses roughly twice as heavily as gains.

---

## Why It Happens

Three forces working together:

**Identity threat.** If I've spent 10 years mastering a process and you replace it, you're not just changing a tool — you're telling me my expertise is obsolete. My competence was my status. Now I'm a beginner again.

**Loss aversion.** Psychologically, losing 100 dollars hurts more than gaining 200 feels good. Change is always framed as "give up the known for the uncertain." The math never feels fair.

**Mere exposure effect.** We prefer things simply because they're familiar. The current way — however broken — feels safe because it's *known*. The new way could be better, but it could also be a disaster. The status quo has a built-in advantage just by existing.

This is why people say "I'm open to change" and then resist every specific instance of it. In the abstract, change is fine. In the specific — when it touches *my* routine, *my* expertise, *my* comfort — it's a different story.

---

## The Trap

**If you push too hard:** You confirm their fear. Forced change feels like proof that their concerns don't matter, which hardens resistance into identity ("I'm someone who doesn't go along with management's whims").

**If you give up:** Nothing changes. The slow decay continues. Kodak stays comfortable until it doesn't exist.

The trap is thinking this is a logic problem. You present better data, clearer arguments, more compelling charts. But you're arguing with *emotion* using *reason*. It's like trying to extinguish a fire by explaining the chemistry of combustion.

---

## The Move

1. **Name the loss.** Before selling the gain, acknowledge what people are giving up. "I know this means re-learning something you're already good at. That's real." Unacknowledged loss becomes underground resistance.

2. **Make the first step tiny.** Not "we're migrating everything." Instead: "Try it for one task this week." Lower the stakes below the threshold that triggers identity threat.

3. **Let the early adopters be proof.** Don't argue theoretically. Let one team go first, succeed visibly, and become the evidence. People trust peers more than presentations.

4. **Protect status during transition.** The person who was the expert in the old system? Make them the guide in the new one. Give their competence a new home instead of deleting it.

5. **Give it time, but not infinite time.** Set a clear horizon. "We're running both in parallel for 6 weeks, then we switch." Ambiguity feeds resistance; deadlines force adaptation.

## Reflection

- What change am I currently resisting — and what's the loss I'm actually afraid of?
- When I've driven change that failed — was I addressing the logic or the emotion?
- Where in my life am I the prisoner defending the shadows?
