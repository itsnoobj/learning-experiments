# Chapter Template

> Every chapter answers one question: "I'm facing X — what do I do?"

## Structure

### 1. The Situation
- 2-4 sentences, second person ("You're in a meeting when...")
- Workplace OR life/relationship — whatever fits the topic
- Instantly relatable — the reader should think "this is happening to me right now"

### 2. The Story (Primary)
- 300-500 words
- From any tradition: mythology, fables, biography, novel, philosophy, history
- Focus on the **moment of decision**, not the full plot
- Give enough context that a reader unfamiliar with the source still gets it
- Vivid, concise storytelling — show, don't lecture

### 3. The Contrast (Secondary Story)
- 100-150 words
- Different tradition from the primary story
- Same principle, different angle — reinforces through variety
- Can be a brief anecdote, a quote-in-context, or a compressed parable

### 4. The Principle
- 2-3 sentences max
- Name the mental model or concept clearly
- Make it quotable — something the reader can carry in their head

### 5. Why It Happens
- 150-250 words
- The psychology: cognitive biases, emotional drivers, social dynamics
- Some depth — not just naming the bias but *why* the brain does this
- Connect the abstract to the felt experience

### 6. The Trap
- 100-150 words
- What goes wrong if you follow the principle too rigidly
- What goes wrong if you ignore it entirely
- The nuance that prevents the lesson from becoming a bumper sticker

### 7. The Move
- Practical, actionable steps
- Numbered list or short checklist
- What to do *this week* if you're in the situation described
- Concrete enough to act on, flexible enough to adapt

### 8. Reflection
- 2-3 questions
- No right answer — designed to make the reader sit with the tension
- Personal and specific, not abstract

### 9. Connections
- One-line links to related chapters
- Shows that human dynamics are interconnected, not siloed

---

## Guidelines

### Length
~1200-1500 words per chapter

### Voice & Tone
- **Situations**: Second person ("You notice that...")
- **Stories**: Third person, narrative
- **Analysis**: First person plural or direct address ("Here's what's actually happening...")
- **Overall**: Conversational but precise — a wise friend explaining over coffee, not a professor lecturing

### Language
- Articulate but not academic
- No jargon walls
- A smart 25-year-old reads it without a dictionary
- Prefer short sentences for punch, longer ones for flow — vary rhythm

### Story Selection
Pick whatever source tells it **best** for that problem:
- Indian epics (Mahabharata, Ramayana, Panchatantra, Jataka tales)
- Greek/Roman (mythology, Stoic texts, Aesop)
- Eastern (Sufi parables, Zen koans, Tao Te Ching)
- Biographies (Lincoln, Gandhi, Mandela, Kalam, Frankl, Munger)
- Business (Grove, Bezos, Dalio anecdotes)
- Novels & Literature (when a fictional scene captures it perfectly)
- Folk tales, fables from any culture

### Story Quality Rules

1. **No logic gaps.** If a reader can ask "but wait, how would they know that?" — the story needs tightening. Every cause→effect must be obvious without the reader needing to assume.

2. **Simple beats clever.** A monkey that won't climb a ladder beats an abstract cave allegory. The reader should *see* it instantly — no philosophy degree required.

3. **Visceral over intellectual.** The best stories create a mental image in one sentence. An elephant chained since childhood, five monkeys beaten for reaching up — these land in the gut, not just the head.

4. **No translation needed.** The story should work for someone in Bangalore, Berlin, or Boston. Avoid references that require cultural context to decode.
   - No local currency symbols — use "100 dollars" or just "a small amount" not ₹ or £
   - When using stories from any tradition (Mahabharata, Greek myths, Sufi tales), briefly introduce who the characters are and where they come from. "In the Indian epic Mahabharata, there's a prince named Arjuna who..." — never assume the reader knows the source.
   - Avoid idioms or metaphors that only work in one culture

5. **The lesson is obvious.** If you have to explain "and the moral of the story is..." the story didn't work. The reader should feel the principle before you name it.

6. **One clear moment.** Focus on the single decision/moment that reveals the principle. Don't retell the full plot — zoom into the frame that matters.

### What Makes a Good Chapter
- [ ] The situation makes you think "I know this feeling"
- [ ] The story is memorable — you'd retell it to a friend
- [ ] The principle has a name you can recall later
- [ ] The psychology makes you go "oh, *that's* why"
- [ ] The move is something you can do tomorrow
- [ ] At least one line is quotable/highlightable

### What to Avoid
- Academic tone or excessive citations
- Exhaustive lists ("here are 15 biases...")
- Moralizing or preaching
- Forcing sources that don't naturally fit
- Oversimplifying into "just do X"
